Why Isn't Yellowstone the Most Visited National Park?

How access explains national park visitation and where it fails short, 1979–2023

Muyu Lin & Yunuo Hu · SI 649

The Expectation

The Most Visited Park Isn't the One You'd Guess

Ask anyone to name America's most popular national park, and you'll hear Yellowstone, Yosemite, the Grand Canyon — parks that define the brand. Vast, pure nature, and iconic.

The data tells a different story.

Great Smoky Mountains has averaged nearly 10 million recreation visits per year since 1979, more than double the Grand Canyon and triple Yellowstone. It has been #1 for four decades.

Fame alone does not explain visitation. So what does?

The Hypothesis

Does Proximity Explain It?

If the Smokies' dominance isn't about fame, maybe it's about access. Great Smoky Mountains sits within driving distance of major population centers like Atlanta and Nashville. Yellowstone requires a flight or a multi-day road trip for most Americans.

A simple linear model (visits predicted by nearby population) confirms a positive but weak relationship. Proximity sets a baseline of demand, but the interesting story is in the residuals: which parks attract substantially more or fewer visitors than their location would predict?

Great Smoky Mountains sits far above the expected line, even among parks with strong nearby access. Yellowstone also overperforms despite having very little nearby population. Together, they show that high visitation is not driven by access alone.

The Structure

A Geography of Access

The residual analysis reveals which parks outperform expectations, but not where they are. The map below adds spatial context and introduces a descriptive typology based on population proximity and visitation volume:

Gateway

Near large populations (≥ 5M within 300 km) and high visitation (≥ 1M/year). These parks benefit from structural proximity with drive-in access from major metros.

Destination

Lower nearby population but still high visitation. Visitors travel specifically for these parks, which are iconic landscapes that draw despite remoteness.

Niche

Lower visitation, often remote or specialized. This isn't a judgment of quality. Many of these parks are extraordinary but structurally harder to reach.

This typology is descriptive, not causal. Nearby population is approximated using the largest U.S. cities (top 300) as a proxy for access, rather than full metropolitan populations. This may underrepresent suburban and regional population catchments and can be sensitive to the chosen radius and city cutoff. As a result, classifications should be interpreted as approximations of access patterns rather than precise measures of true population reach.

The Takeaway

It's Not About Which Park Is Best

Great Smoky Mountains' dominance is not mysterious once you see the structure. It sits near several large population centers, is free to enter, and remains open year-round. Yellowstone is the archetype of a Destination park: remote, seasonal, and expensive to reach.

Visitation numbers are often read as a measure of a park's importance or appeal. In reality, they also reflect where people already are and how easy it is to get in. However, even though access explains some visitation, some parks draw far more people than access alone would predict, pointing to factors like iconic status and seasonal access.
